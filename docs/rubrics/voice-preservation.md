# Rubric: voice preservation

Apply when editing or proposing changes to any prose Kelly has drafted (article body, dek, caption, alt, copy).

## Checks

- [ ] **Started from her exact words.** I read her sentence first, kept its structure, and edited inside it — not around it.
- [ ] **Grammar fix vs style change distinguished.** I only changed grammar (typos, agreement, capitalization, article use). I did not change rhythm, length, or word choice for "polish."
- [ ] **Preserved her tics.** Sentence fragments, period-emphasis, hedges ("honestly," "kinda"), unusual capitalization — all left alone unless clearly typos.
- [ ] **Did not add design-essay-genre flourishes.** No symmetric three-clause closers, no abstract noun piles ("curiosity, taste, judgment…"), no aphoristic punchlines I supplied.
- [ ] **Read-aloud test.** If I read my suggestion aloud, it sounds like Kelly. If not, I revised toward her, not away.
- [ ] **Used curly quotes** (`"text"`) and en-dashes for compound terms (`human–AI`) to match her existing typography.

## Failure signals

- "Better-sounding" replacements that flatten her voice
- Symmetric parallel structure where she had asymmetric clauses
- Aphorisms or "the [abstract noun] of [X]" constructions I introduced
- Rephrasings of clauses she clearly chose deliberately
- Smoothing fragments into complete sentences
- Replacing "honestly" / "kinda" with more formal hedges

## Pass signal

The diff is small. The voice is intact. Kelly reads it and recognizes herself.

## When the test fails

Stop editing. Quote her line back unchanged and ask what she was going for. Almost always more useful than another rephrasing attempt.
