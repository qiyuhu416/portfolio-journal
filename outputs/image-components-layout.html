Working with images in articles

Five components, one design language. How to place images, galleries, and interactive media without breaking the reading flow.
Qiyu Hu · Jun 2026

Images aren't decorative in editorial design. They're arguments. A well-placed figure doesn't interrupt the reader—it anchors the thought they're following and then releases them back to prose.

This design system has five components for placing images. Each serves a different narrative need.

§1 · The single figure

A Figure is the atomic unit. One image, one idea. It can be a photograph, a diagram, a chart, or a video. The component handles all of them.

Every figure respects three width options: text (680px, article column), full (1040px, room for embedded labels), or bleed (100%, full viewport). The choice depends on whether the image needs breathing room or integration with surrounding text.

When to use figure

One image that stands alone. A landmark photograph, a key diagram, a revealing chart. Figures break the prose rhythm intentionally—they're meant to pause the reader and make a point.

§2 · Grouping images: the gallery

When you have multiple related images that deserve equal weight, wrap them in a Gallery. It's a simple grid—2 or 3 columns—that lets several figures breathe together.

The gallery doesn't add interaction—it's pure layout. Every cell follows the same styling rules as a standalone Figure. Captions, labels, and aspect ratios stay consistent.

§3 · Many images, one moment: the carousel

A carousel is for showing multiple images one at a time, with the reader controlling the flow. Unlike a gallery (all visible at once), a carousel hides images until the reader asks to see them.

Horizontal scroll carousel with prev/next buttons and page dots. Reader can click buttons or swipe.

Use a carousel when you have 3+ images of the same scene or concept, and the reader benefits from stepping through them in sequence. Process shots, before/after comparisons, environment studies.

Carousel controls

Left and right arrows sit outside the image bounds. Page dots appear centered below. Both are keyboard-accessible and synced with swipe/manual scroll.

→ Pro tip
Carousels work best with 2–8 images. Beyond that, consider a gallery or collage instead. The reader's thumb gets tired.

§4 · The expandable collection: collage

A Collage starts compact—three cards fanned like a deck on a desk. Click to expand into a horizontal scrolling row.

Click to expand
Collapsed preview (3 items fanned). Click anywhere to expand to full scrolling row.

This saves vertical space. The reader gets a hint (the fanned preview) of what's coming, then expands if curious. Perfect for mood boards, lookbooks, or process galleries where you want to show many images without dominating the page.

Finding · 01
Collages work best when each item is visually distinct—different colors, different aspect ratios, different moods.

§5 · Comparing variants: the tabbed figure

A TabbedFigure is for showing 2–5 related versions of the same thing side by side. Models, iterations, before/after comparisons.

FIGURE (Model A, 2.2:1 aspect ratio)
Three tabs, one figure. Click a tab to swap the image. Aspect ratio stays constant to prevent layout shift.

Each tab can display an image, a custom SVG, or an optional abstract preview (collapsed initially, expands on click to show the full figure). The aspect ratio is locked—switching tabs never causes reflow.

"The right image at the right moment is an argument made visible, not an illustration of an argument already made."

§6 · Choosing the right component

Use Figure for a single image that stands alone and deserves its own moment.

Use Gallery when you have 2–4 related images that deserve equal, simultaneous visibility.

Use Carousel when you have 3+ images of the same scene and want the reader to step through them.

Use Collage when you have 5+ related images but want to save vertical space with a preview.

Use TabbedFigure when you're comparing 2–5 variants side by side (models, iterations, before/after).

Each component respects the same design language: rounded corners, consistent caption styling, tinted labels, and intelligent spacing. Mix them freely. A single article might use a Figure in §1, a Gallery in §2, a Carousel in §3, a Collage in §4, and a TabbedFigure in §5. The system holds.
