<h2 id="setup">§1 · Not good at visuals</h2>

I used to work at an early-stage startup — Meetfood — building a design system from scratch. I remember the team spending a whole meeting discussing the shade of orange. The font. The spacing.

I really hated it.

And from then on, I kind of told people: *"I'm not good at visuals. I don't want to work on B2C products."*

<h2 id="rephrase">§2 · What visuals actually do</h2>

But that wasn't quite right.

What I actually disliked were visuals made for the goal of *polishing* — visuals that existed to look considered, not to communicate.

What I like are visuals that carry information beyond *"it's detail-oriented."* Spacing tells you how things are grouped. Font tells you hierarchy. Color tells you what to attend to first. Visuals themselves are a language.

What I wanted, I realized, was to work on **informative functional visuals**.

<h2 id="clarity">§3 · Details, in service of clarity</h2>

Apple is famous for being detail-oriented. I remember reading about the new CEO — *"this man paid attention to the nails on the Apple display that users wouldn't even see, but he did. That's how he got attention from Tim Cook."*

I think that was oversimplifying the question.

The real question isn't *detail vs. no detail*. It's what those details are serving.

Good visuals aren't about perfection. They're about clarity.

<Aside>
Just like in relationships — depth isn't about how many interactions you have, but what those interactions build over time. Time compounds meaning.
</Aside>
