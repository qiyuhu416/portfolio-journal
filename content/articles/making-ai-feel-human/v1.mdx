<h2 id="relationship">§1 · Designing the relationship</h2>

<PullQuote attribution="a note I keep returning to">
  Designing human–AI interaction is designing the human–AI *relationship*.
</PullQuote>

The interaction is the surface. The relationship is the shape underneath — who defers to whom, who interrupts, who learns from whose mistakes, who ends the conversation. That's the layer I've been trying to design for.

<h2 id="question">§2 · A question I can't shake</h2>

At Apple, I ran a workshop for my design team fellows on designing *with* Claude Code. I was genuinely excited. The team… wasn't. Someone asked, very seriously: *what do you think the value of designers should be, then?*

That made me sad. The question has been in my head ever since.

For a while I worked at an early-stage startup where I was wired directly into the codebase. I shipped to staging myself; my changes got merged to main every week. The team was all engineers — and they wanted a designer. Not despite AI. Because of it. The hands that could move between the two modes were the bottleneck, not either mode alone.

You have to put yourself in an environment like that to feel the answer. Reading about it isn't the same.

<Aside>
  The worry isn't "AI replaces designers." It's "designers don't step into the seat AI leaves open."
</Aside>

<h2 id="cant">§3 · What AI can't do</h2>

Once I spent enough time collaborating with Claude, the limits became specific.

**It doesn't stop.** Prompt Claude to *"act as a world-class designer, critique the work, and then give suggestions based on the critique"* and it will keep going. It will critique, suggest, critique the suggestion, suggest again. Someone still has to decide *enough*. That someone is still me.

**It generates options, not judgments.** A navbar can be designed a hundred good ways. Which one is right depends on whether the tabs have dependencies between them, whether users navigate by keyboard, whether one client hits a particular button six times a day out of habit. I have a client who lives on the keyboard — Claude would never have thought of that, because it has never watched them work.

**It doesn't feel the consequences.** It generates; it doesn't live inside the system afterwards. It doesn't notice a week later that the pattern it suggested is quietly producing support tickets. It doesn't experience the system over time. It doesn't pick up the subtle signals from a person — the hesitation, the habit, the context behind a decision.

And even humans don't always understand themselves. There's a gap between what a person does and what they *meant* to do. That gap — between behavior and intention — is where design still matters. AI fills the fat middle of the distribution. The edges, where the real relationships live, are still ours.
