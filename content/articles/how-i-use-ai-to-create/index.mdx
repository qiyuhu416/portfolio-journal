<h2 id="thinking">
  Don’t let AI bypass your thinking
</h2>

<p>
A lot of things about AI are unteachable lessons — just go and try it out. And you may find it addictive.
</p>

<Aside>
In May 2025, I was hired as an “AI prototyper” for Apple. At the time, other teams thought this sounded very unique. But in just one year (May 2026 now), not using AI enough doesn’t seem to be an issue for most people anymore.
</Aside>

<p>
The real problem seems to be how to face our FOMO, and find the space where AI leaves room.
</p>

<p>
So sharing two things I’ve said in sharing sessions:
</p>

<h3>1. You either use AI to amplify your thinking, or become the human agent executing for AI.</h3>

<p>
There were moments where I would prompt my Claude Code:
</p>

<blockquote>
  “You are a world-class designer. Analyze this problem, give me options, critique those options, and improve them based on your critique.”
</blockquote>

<p>
Honestly, that feels awful. I was too eager to want that productivity, to trade my own brain. And sometimes we took a long loop, going back to the original point. Intentionally. Practice. To. urge. Your. impulse. To. get. That. efficiency.
</p>

<h3>2. Don’t chase the tool</h3>

<p>
One funny thing about AI tools I saw from a meme:
</p>

<blockquote>
  If you learn AI slowly enough, sometimes you realize you didn’t even need to learn the majority of them.
</blockquote>

<p>
Things are moving so fast.
</p>

<p>
For a while everyone talked about prompt engineering, Claude Code tricks (e.g. <code>/compact</code>, <code>skills.md</code>). Then a few months later, the product abstracts half of it away:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>stuff gets baked into the models</li>
  <li>automatic context handling</li>
</ul>

<p>
Right now: sometimes I use tools like Stitch for fast brainstorming. Sometimes I go back to Figma because visual sketches help the LLM understand direction better. Sometimes I prototype directly in code.
</p>

<p>
Languages change. Frameworks change. Interfaces change.
</p>

<p>
You see, this whole process is a prototype. We are all in the prototyping phase of human–AI interaction.
</p>

<hr />


<h2 id="prototype">
  Prototype = Research Mindset
</h2>
<blockquote>
Sometimes we keep using a solution not because it is the best one, but because we’ve used it for a long time.
</blockquote>
<p>
The word "prototype"  <InlineLink to="think-outside-the-box"> might have been automatically associated with </InlineLink> a digital phone frame where people can click around. But we could actually expand the scope here: **As creation itself becomes easier, prototypes can be any form of experiments used to test “what to create.”**
</p>

<p>
And also as prototypes become part of thinking, I don’t think everything should become a coded prototype either. Prototypes can be anything that serves those goals:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>explore more directions</li>
  <li>test more interactions</li>
  <li>simulate more edge cases</li>
  <li>communicate ideas that used to be too expensive to build</li>
  <li>much more</li>
</ul>

<p>
Once creation becomes cheaper, we can rethink whether the existing artifact is even the right artifact anymore.
</p>

<p>
A lot of workflows exist simply because we inherited them.
</p>

<table>
  <thead>
    <tr>
      <th>What we’ve been doing</th>
      <th>Underlying goal</th>
      <th>Given the current tech, is there a better solution?</th>

  </thead>

  <tbody>

    </tr>
  </tbody>
</table>



<hr />


<p>
So where NOT to prototype?
</p>

<p>
Maybe to 100% duplicate a screen you’ve already made somewhere else, or to use a vibe-coding tool to fine-tune visual details. In those cases — why use a second tool, or why not just stick with the original one?
</p>

<p>
Prototypes are not production. Prototypes are expected to be built, not used (unless). Sometimes the fastest way to understand an interaction is to actually build it.
</p>

<h2 id="pitfalls">
  Where to NOT use AI
</h2>

<p>
May 5 is an interesting day I want to mark, as we are now discussing AI fatigue and where not to use AI. I think it might mean a turning point for something.
</p>

<Figure fig="01" src={aiFatigue} alt="A two-column table titled ‘I feel… / When AI is used for…’ pairing feelings (Bypassed, Reluctant to decide, Distanced, Not human, Don’t want AI here, Drained) with quoted reasons people gave for each." caption="AI fatigue, by feeling — collected from people I’ve been talking with." />

<p>
If you’ve tried the prompt from the beginning of this article, you probably know what I mean.
</p>

<p>
The loop can go on forever:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>critique</li>
  <li>suggest</li>
  <li>critique the suggestion</li>
  <li>suggest again</li>
</ul>

<p>
Endlessly.
</p>

<p>
Someone still has to decide what is enough — and that part still feels deeply human to me.
</p>

<p>
Technology is changing, and humans are adapting. But inherently we are not changed that much. We see this in human evolution — many human natures are still “human natures”:
</p>

<p>
Like, curiosity. Be curiosity-driven. And embrace human–human connection!
</p>
