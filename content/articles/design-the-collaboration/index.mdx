UX isn&rsquo;t a job title. It&rsquo;s a mindset — and the mindset doesn&rsquo;t switch off when the work moves from *the product* to *the team making it.*

Just like designing a product or service, cross-functional collaboration also requires understanding the users (in this case, the team members), their needs, their pain points, and their motivations. So I *design* the collaboration — choosing the right frameworks, tools, and rituals to foster alignment, creativity, and efficiency.

Three modes come up again and again. Each one needs a different move.

<h2 id="team">§1 · Within the design team</h2>

Inside the design team, the medium is *drawing.*

When I&rsquo;m thinking through a problem with another designer, we don&rsquo;t begin with a doc. We begin at a whiteboard. Arrows, boxes, half-drawn shapes — the medium *is* the thinking. Writing alone is too linear for a design problem. Drawing together lets two people see the same thing at the same time.

<Figure fig="01" placeholder alt="Two designers at a whiteboard, sketching and writing together" caption="Communicate through collaborative drawing and writing." />

<h2 id="cross">§2 · With a cross-functional team</h2>

With engineers, PMs, and others, the medium shifts. We need shared *structure* before we need shared drawings.

My default move: a goal-breakdown activity. Lay out current state, jobs-to-be-done, pain points, short-term goals, long-term goals. Everyone moves sticky notes into columns. By the time the board is full, people aren&rsquo;t arguing about the *what* anymore — they can see it. They&rsquo;re arguing about the *how*, which is the right argument to have.

<Figure fig="02" placeholder alt="A FigJam goal-breakdown board with rows of sticky notes, colour-coded by column" caption="Goal breakdown activity with the whole team." />

<h2 id="client">§3 · With an external client</h2>

Clients are the hardest *user* to design for — because the team I&rsquo;m collaborating with isn&rsquo;t used to thinking in user-centered terms. So the workshop itself becomes the tool that teaches empathy.

Two formats I keep reaching for:

- **Role-Playing** — you be the user, I be the support rep. Now swap. It&rsquo;s awkward for exactly the right length of time.
- **Rose, Bud, Thorn** — what&rsquo;s working, what&rsquo;s emerging, what&rsquo;s painful. A soft frame that lets anyone in the room — client, engineer, designer — speak in the language of the user without feeling foolish.

<Figure fig="03" placeholder alt="Workshop room: a mixed group seated in a loose circle, one person speaking" caption="Workshops like Role-Playing and Rose, Bud, Thorn — user-centered as a team." />

---

Three modes, three sets of frameworks. But the mindset underneath is the same: **look at your collaborators the way you look at your users. Notice what helps them think. Design for that.**
