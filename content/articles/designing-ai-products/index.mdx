<h2 id="relationship">§1 · Designing the relationship</h2>

<p><strong>Since when did "humans" become a frequent word in our conversations?</strong></p>

<p>
    Pre-AI, we didn’t say “humans” this much — we were just... in it.
    But once AI entered the room, we started seeing ourselves from the outside.
</p>

<PullQuote>
  Designing human–AI interaction is designing the human–AI relationship.
</PullQuote>

<p>
If we look at the evolution of HCI, we see it has always been about relationships:
</p>

Interestingly, the interface is kind of back to terminal again — thanks to tools like Claude Code. But the relationship has changed a lot.

So for me, designing human–AI interaction is not just about UI. It's about defining the relationship first — is AI a tool, assistant, teammate, teacher, agent, or something else? Then design the interaction around that.

<h3 id="hard-inference">Hard inference: elder care AI</h3>

<p>
One research project I worked on at CARE started with this question:
how might an AI messenger support older adults when they need help?
</p>

<p>
It sounds simple, but the relationship is actually very tricky.
</p>

<ul>
  <li>Who should the AI listen to — the older adult or the caregiver?</li>
  <li>Who should get access to sensitive data, like a living will?</li>
  <li>When is an older adult cognitively capable of updating sensitive information?</li>
</ul>

<p>
No one knows the answer from the outside.
So we went to the care center and talked to older adults directly:
15+ interviews, storyboards of potential human-bot interactions, affinity clustering, and flow mapping.
</p>

<Figure fig="02" placeholder alt="Trust logic" caption="How humans build trust → translated into AI behavior." />

<PullQuote>
  We didn't design the answer. We designed a way to learn what the answer should be.
</PullQuote>

<p>
One thing I learned: trust can be designed.
We may not be able to control how older adults feel, but we can design how AI behaves, explains, pauses, asks, and hands off — just like a trustworthy human would.
</p>

<h2 id="errors">§2 · Designing for errors</h2>

AI is never perfect. The design question is: where are we okay with it being wrong?
A lot of human–AI relationships are based on human–human relationships.

<p><strong>BUT HUMANS ARE COMPLICATED. And human–human relationships are even more complicated.</strong></p>

</p>

<h3 id="easy-inference">Easy inference: phone call agent</h3>

<p>
Another project was a phone call agent that answers repetitive customer questions.
This was an easier inference problem: more rule-based, more scriptable, easier to prototype.
I was the solo prototyper for this.
</p>

<p>
But even simple AI still has unknown errors.
So the goal of the prototype was not just to prove the happy path.
It was to surface the known-unknown failures.
</p>

<Figure fig="03" placeholder alt="Phone agent" caption="Prototype to expose failure modes." />

<p>
A 1-hour client testing session already surfaced issues we didn’t expect:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>voice recognition breaks in noisy environments</li>
  <li>users change their mind mid-flow</li>
  <li>some users reject AI entirely: “let me talk to a human”</li>
</ul>

<PullQuote>
  Prototype the relationship early, so the errors show up before the product ships.
</PullQuote>

<h2 id="feeling">§3 · Design the feeling</h2>

<PullQuote>
  What matters is often the unseen part.
</PullQuote>

<p>
Apple says "Design is how it feels."
It sounds nice, but it is actually so hard to design — because feeling is human, personal, and contextual.
</p>

<p>
For AI, the feeling often comes from small interaction choices:
when it speaks, when it waits, when it interrupts, when it disappears.
</p>

<p>
One case study: when I was a vibe coder at a startup, I prototyped a human–AI co-writing tool.
The AI’s goal was to reactively correct errors as users wrote.
</p>

<PullQuote>
  The writer shouldn’t think about the AI. They should just feel supported and write better.
</PullQuote>

<p>
But “writing” is not one action. It has many tiny moments:
</p>

<ul>
  <li>you think about what to write</li>
  <li>you get ready to write</li>
  <li>you type while watching your sentence form</li>
  <li>you pause and think about the next sentence</li>
  <li>you decide whether to edit, continue, or move on</li>
</ul>

<p>
So the trigger matters a lot.
</p>

<div style={{
  margin: '32px 0',
  padding: '22px 26px',
  background: 'var(--surface)',
  borderRadius: 10,
  border: '1px solid var(--line)',
}}>
  <div style={{
    fontFamily: 'var(--mono)', fontSize: 10, letterSpacing: 1.6,
    textTransform: 'uppercase', color: 'var(--article-tint, var(--ink-3))',
    marginBottom: 14,
  }}>
    Trigger → Relationship → Feeling
  </div>

  <ul style={{ margin: 0, paddingLeft: 0, listStyle: 'none' }}>
    <li style={{ marginBottom: 14, lineHeight: 1.5 }}>
      <b>Every 1 second</b> — AI feels like a strict teacher. There is always an eye on you.
    </li>
    <li style={{ marginBottom: 14, lineHeight: 1.5 }}>
      <b>After 3 seconds of pause</b> — AI assumes you finished thinking. But maybe your mind already moved on.
    </li>
    <li style={{ marginBottom: 0, lineHeight: 1.5 }}>
      <b>After clicking away</b> — AI gives you space, but the suggestion might come too late.
    </li>
  </ul>
</div>

<p>
Different triggers create different relationships.
Is AI leading? Waiting? Watching? Interrupting? Quietly supporting?
</p>

<p>
I prototyped each option and asked people to use them while thinking out loud.
Each person felt it differently.
Some users wanted AI to lead: start drafting, then let them edit.
Others wanted AI to wait and only suggest when called.
</p>

<p>
That led me to personal-level personalization:
AI should learn the user’s preference through their unspoken reactions to its suggestions.
Not just what users click — but when they ignore, accept, hesitate, or rewrite.
</p>

<PullQuote>
  Design the relationship. Design for the errors. Design the feeling.
</PullQuote>
