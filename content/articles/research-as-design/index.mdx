<h2 id="separation">§1 · The old separation</h2>

For a long time I believed research happened *before* design. You'd listen, you'd synthesize, you'd hand over the insights, and then the designer would go make something. Clean handoff. Clean separation of concerns.

It's also how you end up with research nobody uses and designs nobody trusts.

<Figure fig="01" placeholder caption="The handoff, pictured honestly — forthcoming." />

<h2 id="mixing">§2 · What mixing them unlocked</h2>

When I started sketching *during* interviews — not afterward — three things changed:

- The sketches became questions. I could show them and ask "is this what you mean?" and get a real answer.
- The research stopped being a document. It became a conversation that kept going.
- I stopped discovering the wrong things six weeks later.

<PullQuote attribution="#22 · a UX lead, Jan 2024">
  Research is just listening with slightly better manners.
</PullQuote>

<h2 id="method">§3 · A method, not a phase</h2>

Research isn't a phase. It's a posture. You can hold it while you sketch, while you prototype, while you ship — and you should, because the user keeps learning too.

The people I trust most on design teams are the ones who never stopped being curious about the problem.
