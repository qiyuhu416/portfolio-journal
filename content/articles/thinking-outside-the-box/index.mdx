But then I realized thinking creatively is actually not hard. Everyone has the ability to come up with creative ideas — I've seen super creative ideas in <InlineLink to="friends-of-friends-at-work">brainstorming sessions</InlineLink> from people from cross-functional teams, anyone.

<PullQuote>
  The hard part is building a structured system that helps you *consistently* think outside the box. and being a UX designer is to practice this mindset to build this system.
</PullQuote>

UX is a mindset - an iterative mindset of figuring out what the problem is and diverging to explore solutions to solve it. Once you have this mindset, you can apply to different places to design anything. You can apply the UX mindset to 
- <InlineLink to="design-the-collaboration">design the XF collaboration </InlineLink> 
- design self-reflection process (link to what i am good at, not good at, etc)
- design the next-generation of human-AI interaction pattern (to insert link, maybe a section of the paragraph or designing AI products)

I am still learning through the iteration itself, but here are my takeaways so far:

- First, see the box.
- Second, understand the box in the system.
- Third, have the courage to break the box.

<h2 id="see">§1 · See the box</h2>

A box is usually an assumption. The first thing that comes to mind when we hear a word. The association we didn't notice we made.

- Designers → Figma (no longer true)
- Engineers → coding (no longer true neither)
- AI → chatbot
- Portfolio → "hello, I am xxx" and a gallery of case studies

Right now, because of AI, we've already seen some of these changes moving on — like designers using vibe-coding tools to code.

So the first question to solve is "how to see our assumptions" -> it's hard to see yourself clearly from as yourself, so models play a role here. At CMU, I learned some models that can help me think structurely, depending on what problem to want to solve:

At work, when the tasks pass on to me, it is somehow already defined, but it never hurts to re-review it to see whether that's the real problem to solve or **learn from senior people's way of thinking (link to quote)**
Pull yourself out of to see it using models, sometimes you may find the problem behind the problem. 

<h2 id="know">§2 · Understand the box</h2>

However, sometimes you might thought you see the box, but you only see **part** the box. 
So, it is more important to see it inside the system -> to better understand why it is there,and whether it is really a box. 

For example, <InlineLink to="how-i-use-ai-to-create">At a startup, I've worked as a vibe coder</InlineLink>. I directly pushed to staging, which got merged to main every week. Mistakes were reversible.

At bigger companies, the same work is distributed across cross-functional teams.


<PullQuote attribution="me, after learning this the slow way">
  It is okay to do leap frog. But you need to know what the assumption is. 
</PullQuote>

There was a time when i saw the market is using a certain technology, but our team is not using it. 
At first i was kinda not understand why, having talked to the manager many times, then i realized i only have seen part of the system. 

That workflow is not really a box if i put it in the context of the work? Different goals, different workflows. 

But that doesn't mean you stop looking. The courage is in knowing *where*.

<h2 id="break">§3 · Break the box</h2>

This is the hardest part. This is a transition from individual work to making it a group work. 

I often get asked *why?* — changing takes time to experiments, and experimentation doesn't always lead to success (how we define success is another topic).
The proposal won't always get adopted, but that doesn't mean I should not bring it up. 

<Aside>
  In a alumni panel to my fellows at Cornell, I said one underrated value of being a designer is to have resillience lol, you might face may rejections (link to how i face rejections)...
</Aside>

My takeaway: **start small, and make it fun.**

Applying that model thinking we discussed in the previous section, in the design process there are so many opportunities to make it fun.
