<h2 id="see">§1 · See the box</h2>

<PullQuote>
  You can’t question the box if you can’t see it.
</PullQuote>

<p>
A “box” is usually just an assumption —  
the first thing that comes to mind when we hear a word. The association we didn’t notice we made.
</p>

<ul>
  <li>Designers → Figma</li>
  <li>Engineers → coding</li>
  <li>AI → chatbot</li>
  <li>Portfolio → “hello, I am…” + case studies</li>
</ul>

<p>
These used to feel true. Now, because of AI, a lot of them are already shifting. Designers are vibe-coding (which is what I'm doing right now, building this site). Engineers are prototyping interfaces. AI is becoming more than a chatbot.
</p>

<PullQuote>
  Most problems aren’t wrong — they’re just framed too early. 
</PullQuote>

<p>
And, the tricky part is you can’t see your own assumptions from inside them.
</p>

<h2 id="know">§2 · Understand the box</h2>

<PullQuote>
  If you don’t understand the context of where the box lives, you may break the wrong thing.
</PullQuote>

<p>
Sometimes you think you’ve seen the box — but you’ve only seen part of it. That’s why I try to understand the box inside the system: why it exists, who is involved, and whether it is actually a constraint or just a habit.
</p>

<div style={{
  fontFamily: 'var(--mono)', fontSize: 10, letterSpacing: 1.6,
  textTransform: 'uppercase', color: 'var(--article-tint, var(--ink-3))',
  marginTop: 32, marginBottom: 12,
}}>
  Example
</div>

<p>
At a startup, <InlineLink to="how-i-use-ai-to-create">I worked as a vibe coder</InlineLink>.  
I pushed my design in code directly to staging, and staging got merged to main every week. Mistakes were reversible.
</p>

<p>
At a bigger company, the same kind of work sits inside a very different system: more teams, more dependencies, more risk, more coordination.
</p>

<p>
There was a time when I saw the market using a certain technology, but our team wasn’t. At first, I didn’t really get why. After talking to my manager many times, I realized I had only seen part of the system.
</p>

<p>
Different goals, different workflows.
</p>
  
<p>
But that doesn’t mean I stop questioning it. The courage is in knowing where to push.
</p>

<h2 id="break">§3 · Break the box</h2>

<p>
This is the hardest part, because it moves from individual thinking to group work.
</p>

<p>
I often get asked “why?” Why change this? Why try this? Why spend time experimenting? And fair — experimentation does not always lead to success. The proposal won’t always get adopted. But that doesn’t mean I shouldn’t bring it up.
</p>

<Aside>
  In an alumni panel to my fellows at Cornell, I said one underrated value of being a designer is resilience lol. You might face many rejections.
</Aside>

<PullQuote>
  Start small, and make it fun
</PullQuote>

<p><b>Breaking the box together can be very fun:</b></p>

<p>
    In a brainstorming session, I invited my team to roleplay — engineers were the LLM, my manager was the customer. It helped us align before real prototyping. And it was fun.
</p>

<p>
In another project, we had a recruitment problem. So I asked: what if the survey was impossible to ignore? We placed it in strange, funny, unavoidable places.
</p>
