<h2 id="see">☝️ · See the box</h2>

<p>
You can’t question the box if you can’t see it.
</p>

<p>
A “box” is usually just an assumption, the association we didn’t notice we made.
</p>

<Aside>
Just like:
<ul>
  <li>Designers → Figma</li>
  <li>Engineers → coding</li>
  <li>AI → chatbot</li>
  <li>Portfolio → “hello, I am…” + case studies</li>
</ul>

</Aside>


<p>
These used to feel true. Now, because of AI, a lot of them are already shifting (like I'm vibe coding this site using Claude Code now). But how do you spot an assumption before it hardens into a default? 
</p>

<p>
You can’t see your own assumptions from inside them. <strong> Use model thinking to look at things differently. </strong> 
</p>

<p>
You can also start from observation, then work backwards to build your own model.
</p>

<ul>
  <li>Observation & Induction (noticing patterns)</li>
  <li>Deduction (applying a rule to your own case)</li>
  <li>Abduction (proposing a new possible model)</li>
</ul>

<p>
I use this to explore the question: <InlineLink to="how-i-use-ai-to-create">Given AI exists now, is there a better way to achieve the same goal </InlineLink> instead of sticking to workflows we’ve followed for years simply because we got used to them? 
</p>




<h2 id="know">✌️ · Understand the box</h2>

<p>
Sometimes you think you’ve seen the box, but you’ve only seen part of it. 
</p>

<p>
  If you don’t understand the context of where the box lives, you may break the wrong thing.
</p>


<Sidebar label="Same role, different system">
  <ul>
    <li>**At a startup**, <InlineLink to="how-i-use-ai-to-create">I worked as a vibe coder</InlineLink>. I pushed my design in code directly to staging, and staging got merged to main every week. Mistakes were reversible.</li>
    <li>**At Apple**, I am a vibe coder too. However, the same kind of work sits inside a very different system: more teams, more dependencies, more risk, more coordination.</li>
    <li>Different goals, different workflows.</li>
  </ul>
</Sidebar>
  
<p>
Along the way, I learned not only to keep questioning, but also knowing when to push. 
Aspects to look for: why it exists, who is involved, and whether it is actually a constraint or just a habit.
</p>

<h2 id="break">👌 · Break the box</h2>

<p>
This is the hardest part, because it moves from individual thinking to group work.
</p>

<p>
I often get asked “why?” Why change this? Why try this? Why spend time experimenting? And fair, experimentation does not always lead to success. The proposal won’t always get adopted. But that doesn’t mean I shouldn’t bring it up.
</p>

<Aside>
  In Autumn 2025, I participated as an alumni panelist, where I told my fellow designers "one underrated value of being a designer is resilience."  Still applies now in 2026... lol. 
</Aside>

<h3>Start small, and make it fun.</h3>

<p>
In one project, we had a recruitment problem. So I asked: what if the survey was impossible to ignore? 
</p>

<Sidebar label="Small ways I've tried to make work fun">
  <ul>
    <li>**Prototyping doesn't have to be technical**: try paper prototyping in meetings with your engineers roleplaying the LLM.</li>
    <li>**Coding doesn't have to be eng's work**: share a repo with the PM and prototype together.</li>
    <li>**Work doesn't have to be in the office**: invite your peers to go for a walk and brainstorm.</li>
  </ul>
</Sidebar>
